Why It Exists

The Problem With Every Other Metric

"What did the hitter actually do to score runs for his team? No existing public metric answered that question completely."

WAR is a rate-based defensive framework that treats offense as one input among many. wRC+ measures offensive efficiency but ignores damage. OPS gives equal weight to on-base percentage and slugging, which research has consistently shown overvalues slugging. RBI is roster-dependent. None of them charge a player for hitting into a double play. None of them weight caught stealing by how often a player actually succeeds. None of them account for the difference between a catcher and a right fielder producing the same raw numbers.

Bases Gained was built to answer one question completely: what did this hitter and runner contribute to his team's run-scoring effort, net of the damage he caused, in the context of where he plays? Every component in the formula exists because the public alternatives either ignore it or measure it incompletely.

It is not a replacement for wRC+ or WAR. It is a different question with a different answer. The cases where BG and traditional metrics disagree most sharply are the cases most worth examining.

The positional adjustment is not a reward for playing a hard position. It is a reflection of the offensive standard each position has historically demanded among qualified hitters. A catcher producing at the level Raleigh produced in 2025 exceeds the historical expectation for that position by a wide margin. Judge, playing right field, is held to a higher raw standard because right fielders as a group have historically been the most offensively productive position in baseball.

FAQ

Common Questions

Bases Gained (BG) is a proprietary offensive metric developed by The Baseball Nerd. It measures the net positive contribution of a hitter and runner to their team's run-scoring effort. It counts every positive offensive action, applies explicit penalties for double plays and caught stealing, and adjusts for positional context to produce a single number representing what the player actually did to help his team score runs.
A ground ball double play is the single worst outcome a batter can produce. It records two outs and eliminates a baserunner simultaneously. No other outcome does that much damage at once. Most public metrics either ignore GIDP entirely or fail to price its true run expectancy cost. The BG framework applies an explicit penalty to each double play, making it one of the only offensive frameworks that directly charges hitters for rally-killing outcomes.
The caught stealing penalty is weighted by career stolen base success rate. A player who succeeds 75% of the time in his career is penalized less for a caught stealing than a player who succeeds only 30% of the time. The rationale: a high-percentage basestealer getting caught is a more unusual and defensible outcome than a low-percentage basestealer attempting and failing. The penalty reflects the decision quality embedded in the career rate, not just the binary outcome of the single event.
Bases Gained is intentionally not park adjusted. It measures what a player actually contributed in the games he played, not a modeled estimate of what he might have contributed in a neutral environment. Park adjustment is documented as a known limitation in the BG white paper and is a planned future extension. Charlie Blackmon winning 2017 on BG while playing at Coors Field is an honest outcome of this design decision, and the methodology section addresses it directly.
By Bases Gained, yes. Raleigh finished 2025 at 613.45 BG, Judge at 586.10. Raleigh won on non-home run RBI, double play penalty (Judge hit into significantly more GIDP), and the positional adjustment reflecting the offensive standard at catcher versus right field. Judge led on total bases, walks, and RE24. The argument is not that Judge had a bad season. It is that the full ledger, including positional context and damage pricing, produces a different answer than rate-based efficiency metrics alone.

BG Applied

The Framework in the Field

The real test of any metric is what it surfaces when applied to actual teams and actual lineups. These pieces used Bases Gained on real 2026 data to answer questions traditional stats could not.

Lineup Construction 2025
Did Lineup Spot Matter for the 2025 Cubs? BG Breaks It Down Game by Game.
BG calculated game by game, matched to batting order position across a full season. The results were not what Craig Counsell expected.
Pete Crow-Armstrong produced 3.53 BG per game batting 7th. He averaged 2.91 batting 4th, where Counsell played him 61 games.
Kyle Tucker was the only Cub whose primary slot and best production rate aligned. Counsell got exactly one lineup decision right.
Dansby Swanson averaged 3.67 BG per game batting 7th vs. 2.13 batting 5th, over meaningful sample sizes in both slots.
Carson Kelly in slot 5 may be the single worst lineup construction decision of the season. His slot 9 rate was 4.36 BG per game.

Why Are the Kansas City Royals 12-17? BG Finds the Real Problem.
The offensive conversation in Kansas City was misdirected. BG through 29 games showed the bottom of the order was not the problem. The middle was, and the bullpen was worse.
Bobby Witt Jr. at 102.17 BG was generating more than twice the contribution of any other Royals hitter.
