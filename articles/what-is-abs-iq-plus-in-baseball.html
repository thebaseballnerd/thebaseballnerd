What Is ABS IQ+ in Baseball? The Challenge Intelligence Metric Explained.

We built a proprietary score to rank every MLB team and player on how well they use the 2026 Automated Ball-Strike challenge system. Here is what the data reveals, how the score is built, and where every team stands right now.

By Pete Dwyer  |  Published May 2026  |  10 min read
Quick Answer

ABS IQ+ is a proprietary composite metric from The Baseball Nerd scoring how intelligently MLB teams and players use the 2026 Automated Ball-Strike challenge system. Scaled to 100 equals league average, following the wRC+ and ERA+ convention, it measures four things: raw challenge accuracy, skill above what the pitch locations deserved, challenge rate discipline, and net value above the Statcast expected model. Kansas City leads MLB at 113.9. Cleveland sits last at 85.9. That gap compounds over 162 games.

How the ABS Challenge System Works

The Automated Ball-Strike system made its MLB debut on Opening Day 2026. It uses Hawk-Eye camera technology, the same system powering Statcast, to track the precise location of every pitch relative to each batter's individually measured strike zone. The zone is 17 inches wide, matching home plate. The top sits at 53.5 percent of each batter's measured height without cleats. The bottom is at 27 percent. Pitch location is captured at the midpoint of the plate, not the front edge.

Each team receives two challenges per game. Only the active batter, the pitcher, or the catcher can initiate one. No manager involvement, no dugout assistance. The player taps their helmet within two seconds. A successful challenge is retained. A failed challenge costs you one. In extra innings, any team starting an inning without challenges receives one additional challenge for that inning. The system does not apply when a position player is pitching.

The Behavioral Data That Made This Metric Necessary

Six weeks of 2026 data has confirmed a consistent pattern. As game leverage increases, batter challenge rates climb. Catcher challenge rates hold steady. The gap between those two curves is where ABS strategy either wins or loses.

On a full count, batters challenge at nearly four times the rate of catchers and win only a third of the time. Catchers challenge sparingly and win 41 percent in the same situation. The emotional urgency of a potential strikeout is overriding the analytical question of whether the pitch actually missed the zone.

The pattern holds on two-strike pitches as well. Batters challenge 15 percent of the time and convert 39 percent. Fielders challenge 2.5 percent of the time and convert 53 percent. Same count, same stakes, completely opposite results.

How ABS IQ+ Is Built

ABS IQ+ is a four-component composite score. Each component is z-score normalized within position group and scaled to 100 equals league average: one standard deviation above average equals 115.

Raw Accuracy
30%
Won percentage on challenges initiated. Did the pitch actually miss the zone?
Skill Accuracy
20%
Won percentage above Statcast expected overturn rate. Winning on harder pitches than the model predicted signals genuine zone recognition, not just easy calls.
Discipline
30%
Actual challenge rate vs. the Statcast expected model, which accounts for pitch location, challenges remaining, runners on, and count. Over-challenging is penalized.
Net Value
20%
Net challenges gained above expectation using the Statcast probability model. The closest available proxy for run value impact from challenge decisions.

Challenge rate is measured as challenges out of challengeable pitches: called balls or strikes where the player had challenges remaining and did not swing. The expected model was developed by Tom Tango and the MLB data team. Full methodology is in the ABS IQ+ white paper, available in the Data Vault.

What the Numbers Reveal

Kansas City at 113.9 is not built on one exceptional number. Their batters win 62 percent of challenges at a disciplined 3.7 percent rate. Their catchers win 62 percent on the defensive side. Both halves of their operation are running the same framework: challenge when you are right, not when you are desperate. That is organizational intelligence, not individual talent.

Detroit at 107.4 tells a different story. Their batters convert at exactly 50 percent, dead average. Their fielding corps converts at 76 percent, the highest in baseball, almost entirely because of Dillon Dingler. He challenges so rarely and so accurately that Detroit wins the defensive ABS game without needing anything from their lineup. A catcher-first identity.

Houston at 95.3 is the most analytically dissonant result in the leaderboard. Their batters win 58 percent of challenges, third-best in MLB, but challenge at a 5.0 percent rate among the highest in the league. Their fielding corps converts at just 48 percent. They are accurate but undisciplined on offense and their battery is actively losing value on defense. Winning on talent and losing on strategy at the same time.

Colorado at 92.0 challenges at a 6.4 percent batter rate, highest in baseball, and wins only 42 percent. Their discipline score is 66, worst in the league. They are spending challenges like they do not understand what they cost.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is ABS IQ+ in baseball?

ABS IQ+ is a proprietary metric from The Baseball Nerd scoring how intelligently MLB teams and players use the Automated Ball-Strike challenge system. 100 equals league average, following the wRC+ and ERA+ convention. Four components: raw accuracy (30%), skill accuracy above expected (20%), discipline (30%), and net value (20%).

Which team has the highest ABS IQ+ in 2026?

Kansas City Royals at 113.9 through mid-May 2026. Their batters win 62 percent of challenges at a disciplined 3.7 percent rate and their catchers match that 62 percent on the defensive side. Both halves running the same framework.

Why does discipline carry as much weight as accuracy?

On full counts, batters challenge at 24 percent and win only 33 percent. Catchers challenge at 6.5 percent and win 41 percent. Undisciplined challengers consistently show lower overturn rates and negative net value simultaneously. The two failure modes travel together.

What is the difference between won% and xWon%?

Won% is the raw overturn rate on challenges initiated. xWon% is the Statcast expected overturn rate given the specific pitch locations challenged. A player winning 75 percent on pitches expected to overturn at 48 percent is demonstrating genuine zone recognition. The skill accuracy component rewards that gap.
