What is ABS IQ+? The Metric Measuring Baseball's Challenge Intelligence

We built a proprietary score to rank every MLB team and player on how well they use the Automated Ball-Strike system. Here is what the data shows.

By Pete Dwyer  |  The Baseball Nerd

ABS IQ+ is a proprietary metric from The Baseball Nerd that scores how intelligently MLB teams and players use the 2026 Automated Ball-Strike challenge system. Scaled to 100 equals league average, following the convention of wRC+ and ERA+, it measures four things: raw challenge accuracy, skill above what the pitches deserved, challenge rate discipline, and net value generated above the Statcast expected model.

The short version: some teams are dramatically better at this than others, and raw overturn percentage is not the whole story. Kansas City leads MLB at 113.9. Cleveland sits last at 85.9. That 28-point spread represents a real strategic advantage that compounds over 162 games.

This article explains how the ABS challenge system works, what makes a challenge decision smart or reckless, and where every team and player currently stands.

How the ABS Challenge System Works

The Automated Ball-Strike system made its MLB debut on Opening Day 2026. It uses Hawk-Eye camera technology, the same system powering Statcast, to track the precise location of every pitch relative to each batter's individually measured strike zone.

Each team receives two challenges per game. Only the active batter, the pitcher, or the catcher can initiate a challenge. No manager involvement, no assistance from the dugout. The player taps their helmet within two seconds of the call. The result appears on stadium videoboards and broadcasts nearly instantly.

The key rule: A successful challenge is retained. Only a failed challenge costs you one. This means a disciplined team that challenges correctly can theoretically use their two challenges all game. A team that guesses wrong twice loses both before the third inning.

In extra innings, any team that starts an inning without challenges receives one additional challenge for that inning. The system does not apply when a position player is pitching.

The strike zone is 17 inches wide, matching home plate. The top sits at 53.5 percent of each batter's measured height without cleats. The bottom is at 27 percent. The zone is captured above the midpoint of the plate, not the front edge. This is a two-dimensional zone, not the three-dimensional rulebook definition.

What Makes a Smart ABS Challenge?

Six weeks of 2026 data has already revealed a consistent behavioral pattern. As game leverage increases, batter challenge rates rise. Catcher challenge rates stay flat. The gap between those two curves is where ABS strategy either wins or loses.

On a full count, batters challenge at nearly four times the rate of catchers. They win a third of the time. Catchers challenge sparingly and win 41 percent of the time in the same situation. The emotional urgency of a potential strikeout is overriding the analytical question of whether the pitch actually missed the zone.

The same pattern holds on two-strike pitches. Batters challenge 15 percent of the time and convert 39 percent. Fielders challenge 2.5 percent of the time and convert 53 percent. Same count, same stakes, completely opposite results. One side is making a situational call. The other is reacting.

A smart ABS challenge has three properties: the pitch was genuinely borderline, the game situation justifies spending the resource, and the challenger has enough zone awareness to identify the difference between a close pitch and a clear strike. The Statcast expected model, built by Tom Tango and the MLB data team, incorporates pitch location, challenges remaining, runners on base, and the current count and out situation to define what a reasonable challenge looks like. ABS IQ+ measures actual behavior against that baseline.

How ABS IQ+ Is Scored

ABS IQ+ is a four-component composite score. Each component is normalized within position group using z-scores and scaled to the 100=average convention: one standard deviation above average equals 115.

30%

Raw accuracy. Overturn rate on challenges initiated. Did the pitch actually miss the zone?

20%

Skill accuracy. Won percentage above expected win percentage. Winning on harder pitches than the model predicted.

30%

Discipline. Actual challenge rate versus Statcast expected rate. Over-challenging is penalized. The emotional challenge is the primary failure mode.

20%

Net value. Challenges gained above expectation using the Statcast probability model.

Full methodology, component definitions, and scoring procedures are documented in the ABS IQ+ white paper, available in the Data Vault.

What the Numbers Actually Reveal

The best ABS teams are not necessarily the ones with the best hitters. Kansas City's batters win 62 percent of their challenges and challenge at a disciplined 3.7 percent rate. Their catchers match that 62 percent on the defensive side. Both sides of the ball are working from the same framework: challenge when you're right, not when you're desperate.

Detroit is a completely different model. Their batters convert at exactly 50 percent, dead average. Their fielding corps converts at 76 percent, the highest in baseball. Dillon Dingler is the primary reason. He challenges so rarely and so accurately that Detroit can win the defensive ABS game without needing much from their lineup. That is a catcher-first ABS identity.

Houston is the most analytically interesting result in baseball right now. Their batters win 58 percent of challenges, third-best in MLB. But they challenge at a 5.0 percent rate, tied for one of the highest in the league. And their fielding corps converts at just 48 percent, below average. They are accurate but undisciplined on offense, and their battery is actively losing value on defense. That is a team winning on talent and losing on strategy simultaneously.

Colorado is challenging batters at a 6.4 percent rate, highest in MLB, and winning only 42 percent of the time. Their discipline component score is 66, the worst in baseball. They are spending challenges like they do not understand what they cost.
