Contact Suppression
Rate (CSR)

A Composite Pitching Framework for Evaluating Cy Young Candidacy

By Pete Dwyer · The Baseball Nerd Analytics Vault
9 Sections
3-Year Backtest
6 of 6 Cy Young Winners
68 Qualified Starters
Abstract

Contact Suppression Rate (CSR) is a composite pitching metric developed by The Baseball Nerd to evaluate Cy Young candidacy through a lens that existing frameworks fail to fully address. ERA captures results but rewards pitchers playing in front of elite defenses. FIP corrects for defense but strips away the contextual run-prevention information that Cy Young voters correctly incorporate. CSR proposes a third path: combining rate-based contextual run prevention (RE24/IP) with the two most defense-independent signals in modern public data (K-BB% and Barrel%), weighted equally through z-score standardization. Validated against six Cy Young Awards from 2023 to 2025, the winner ranked in the CSR top 4 every single time. Five of six winners ranked in the top 3.

01

Motivation

The Problem ERA Cannot Solve

ERA is the most familiar pitching statistic in baseball and the one most likely to appear on a Cy Young ballot. It is also structurally flawed as a talent measure. ERA includes every run that scores against a pitcher regardless of whether the pitcher, the defense, the park, or random variance in hit distribution was responsible. Research on ERA-to-FIP differences has consistently shown that ERA variance attributable to defense and BABIP luck can account for 30 to 40 percent of the gap between a pitcher's surface result and his true talent level in a single season.

Why FIP Is Insufficient for Cy Young Analysis

FIP corrects for the defense problem by removing all batted ball outcomes and focusing exclusively on strikeouts, walks, and home runs. It is a better measure of long-run true talent than ERA and should be the preferred lens for player evaluation in most contexts. But FIP has a specific limitation when applied to Cy Young analysis: it evaluates pitcher talent in isolation from the actual run-prevention context that voters observe. Cy Young voters see ERA. They see WHIP. They watch a pitcher strand runners across six months of competition. That contextual information is not noise to be corrected away. It is part of the signal the award was designed to recognize.

The Orienting Question

CSR began as an answer to a straightforward challenge: how do you combine contextual run prevention with defense-independent peripherals without the noise of ERA and without the information loss of FIP? The metric that emerged uses three components — a rate-based run-prevention term, a command-and-stuff term, and a contact suppression term — weighted equally through z-score standardization.

02

Methodology

Component 1 — RE24/IP

RE24 (Run Expectancy based on the 24 base-out states) measures the change in run expectancy a pitcher produces across every plate appearance. Unlike ERA, RE24 credits a pitcher for stranding inherited runners and penalizes him for runners he allows to score. Dividing RE24 by innings pitched converts the counting stat into a rate. RE24/IP does incorporate some defense signal — a ball in play that finds a gap reduces the pitcher's RE24. This is intentional. Cy Young voters observe the same contextual information. A pitcher who performs in high-leverage situations, limits damage, and strands runners is demonstrating a real and observable skill set. CSR does not attempt to isolate the pitcher from his environment; it evaluates him within it, then corrects for surface noise through the other two components.

Component 2 — K-BB%

K-BB% is the difference between strikeout rate and walk rate. It is one of the most defense-independent statistics in public data: a strikeout happens entirely between pitcher and batter with no fielder involvement, and a walk is similarly determined before contact occurs. A high K-BB% signals both the ability to miss bats and the command to avoid free passes — two of the most stable pitcher skills across samples. K-BB% is weighted equally to RE24/IP. The argument that K-BB% should receive less weight misunderstands the purpose of the composite. RE24/IP carries the results. K-BB% is the sustainability correction. Equal weighting ensures that strong RE24/IP results built on fragile peripherals do not outscore a pitcher whose results are backed by repeatable, defense-independent skill.

Component 3 — Barrel% (Inverted)

Barrel% measures the percentage of batted ball events classified as barreled by Statcast: exit velocity of 98 mph or higher at a launch angle producing a batting average above .500. It is the most predictive single contact quality metric for run prevention outcomes. Barrel% is inverted before inclusion in CSR — lower barrel rates are better, so the z-score is multiplied by negative one. A pitcher with a 2% barrel rate scores positively on this component; a pitcher with a 12% barrel rate scores negatively. The Barrel% component captures what K-BB% misses: Max Fried ranks below average on K-BB% but leads the 2026 dataset in Barrel% suppression at 1.8%, because his pitch mix produces weak contact through movement rather than through swings and misses.

Qualification Threshold

CSR is computed for all pitchers who meet the expected qualified threshold — one inning pitched for every team game played. This is the same standard applied to ERA qualifiers and aligns with the innings requirement Cy Young voters implicitly apply. For the 2026 season through June 3, the qualified pool contains 68 starting pitchers.

03

Formula

CSR = ( z(RE24/IP) + z(K-BB%) + z(Barrel%inverted) ) / 3
Where z(x) = (x − mean(x)) / sd(x) computed over all qualified starters in the current season.
Barrel% z-score is multiplied by −1 before inclusion so that lower barrel rates score positively.

Z-scoring equalizes the influence of each component regardless of natural scale. RE24/IP, K-BB%, and Barrel% operate on different numeric ranges. Combining them in raw form would allow the component with the widest distribution to quietly dominate the composite. Z-scoring converts each component to standard deviations from the mean, ensuring a one-standard-deviation improvement in Barrel% suppression carries identical weight to a one-standard-deviation improvement in K-BB% or RE24/IP.

Interpretive Scale

A CSR of 0.000 equals exactly pool average across all three components. A score of +1.000 represents a pitcher approximately one standard deviation above average simultaneously in run prevention, command, and contact suppression. Scores above +2.000 are historically rare and represent historically dominant seasons.

06

Case Studies

Misiorowski vs. Sánchez: ERA First vs. CSR First

Cristopher Sánchez leads the qualified pool in ERA at 1.47 and ranks NL 4th in CSR. Jacob Misiorowski ranks ERA 4th at 1.83 but leads CSR at 2.336. The gap is explained almost entirely by K-BB%: Sánchez's z-score is +1.60; Misiorowski's is +3.13, historically dominant. Sánchez's BABIP of .337 is significantly elevated and represents a regression risk. Misiorowski's BABIP of .250 is at the expected range for his contact suppression profile. CSR correctly identifies Misiorowski as the more dominant pitcher independent of where balls have been landing.

Nick Martinez: The RE24 Warning

Nick Martinez (TBR) ranks 3rd in ERA at 1.62. His RE24/IP is the best in the qualified dataset, reflecting genuine in-context run prevention. But his K-BB% ranks near the bottom of the pool, his SIERA is 4.41, and his ERA-xERA gap is −2.52 — xERA expects him to be a full two-and-a-half runs worse. CSR does not include him in the AL top five because his contact suppression and command peripherals cannot sustain his surface ERA. He is not a CSR candidate. He is a regression candidate.

Max Fried: The Barrel Case

Max Fried (NYY) ranks 26th in ERA at 3.21. His K-BB% z-score is −0.57, below average. But his Barrel% of 1.8% is the lowest in the entire qualified dataset. No pitcher is allowing weaker contact. The Barrel% z-score of +2.36 carries his CSR to AL 5th. Contact suppression at that level is a real, repeatable skill. CSR surfaces him as a watch-list candidate that ERA misses — and the historical backtest shows this exact pattern has played out twice in the last two seasons.

The One Miss: Blake Snell 2023

Snell won the 2023 NL Cy Young with a 2.25 ERA and ranked 6th in CSR at +0.927. His FIP was 3.44 — an ERA-FIP gap of 1.19 that was the largest in the entire 2023 qualified pool. His K-BB% z-score was +0.257, barely above average. His RE24/IP was the strongest component of his CSR. But the contact suppression and command peripherals did not support his surface ERA. CSR placed him 6th because the underlying profile was fragile. He did not qualify the following season. The metric was right about what the numbers said.

08

Limitations

  • RE24 is not explicitly park-neutral. Pitchers in hitter-friendly parks will post slightly lower RE24/IP all else equal. The K-BB% and Barrel% components partially correct for this, but the park effect is not fully eliminated.
  • Z-scores are computed relative to the qualified pool in the current season. As the pool changes throughout the year, CSR values will shift even if an individual pitcher's underlying numbers remain constant. Values should be interpreted relative to the published date.
  • Barrel% requires Statcast tracking, available at all 30 MLB venues but not consistently available before the Statcast era. Historical comparisons before 2019 are not reliable.
  • Equal weighting of the three components is a defensible choice, not a derived one. Users who believe one component deserves more weight should note that the z-score architecture makes reweighting straightforward: multiply any component's z-score by the desired weight before averaging.
  • CSR is a within-season relative metric. Cross-season comparisons require recomputation against the relevant season's qualified pool. Use CSR rank rather than raw score for cross-season comparisons.
  • CSR is designed for starting pitchers with sufficient innings to qualify. It is not calibrated for relievers, openers, or two-way players.
  • CSR does not predict injuries. Between 50 and 60 percent of CSR top-10 leaders in a given season do not qualify the following year. This is a fact about elite starting pitchers, not a metric artifact.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is Contact Suppression Rate (CSR)?
Contact Suppression Rate (CSR) is a proprietary composite MLB pitching metric developed by The Baseball Nerd. It combines three components through equal z-score weighting: RE24/IP (contextual run prevention rate), K-BB% (strikeout-minus-walk rate), and Barrel% inverted (contact quality suppression). The formula is CSR = (z(RE24/IP) + z(K-BB%) + z(Barrel%_inverted)) / 3. A score of 0.000 represents pool average. Higher positive scores indicate greater dominance across all three dimensions simultaneously.
How accurate is CSR at predicting Cy Young Award winners?
Across six Cy Young Awards from 2023 to 2025, the winner ranked in the CSR top 4 every single time. Five of the six winners ranked in the CSR top 3. The one exception was Blake Snell in 2023, whom CSR correctly identified as having the most fragile peripheral profile in the NL pool based on an ERA-FIP gap of 1.19 runs — the largest in the qualified dataset. He did not qualify the following season.
Why does CSR use RE24 instead of a fully defense-independent metric?
CSR is designed specifically for Cy Young evaluation, not pure talent isolation. Cy Young voters observe contextual run prevention: ERA, WHIP, strand rate, high-leverage performance across a full season. RE24/IP captures that same information set as a rate stat. The K-BB% and Barrel% components provide the defense-independent corrections. A metric that strips all batted ball context answers the wrong question for Cy Young analysis: the question is not who is best in a vacuum, but who most deserves the award voters will actually give based on the performance they can observe.
How is CSR different from FIP, xFIP, or SIERA?
FIP, xFIP, and SIERA are defense-independent ERA estimators designed to measure true pitching talent by removing batted ball outcomes. CSR deliberately includes contextual run prevention (RE24/IP) while adding defense-independent corrections (K-BB% and Barrel%). They answer different questions. CSR and ERA estimators are complementary tools: CSR uses FIP, xFIP, and SIERA as cross-reference inputs to validate rankings and flag regression candidates.
Why use z-scores instead of a simpler weighting formula?
Z-score standardization equalizes the influence of each component regardless of its natural scale. RE24/IP, K-BB%, and Barrel% operate on different numeric ranges. If combined raw, the component with the widest distribution would quietly dominate the composite regardless of the divisor used. Z-scoring ensures that a one-standard-deviation improvement in Barrel% suppression carries identical weight to a one-standard-deviation improvement in K-BB% or RE24/IP. Any constant divisor — dividing by 3, by 7, by any number — produces identical rankings. Only z-scoring solves the actual weighting problem.
Who leads the 2026 Cy Young Watch by CSR?
As of June 3, 2026, Jacob Misiorowski (MIL) leads the National League with a CSR of +2.336 — the highest score in the current qualified pool, comparable to Chris Sale's 2024 Cy Young season (+2.126). Cam Schlittler (NYY) leads the American League at +1.255. The live leaderboard updates weekly at thebaseballnerd.com/pitching-analysis-2026.html.
Can CSR scores be compared across seasons?
CSR is a within-season relative metric. Because z-scores are calculated against each season's specific qualified pool, a score of +1.200 in 2026 does not mean exactly the same thing as +1.200 in 2024. Within a season, rankings and score gaps are fully meaningful. For cross-season comparisons, use CSR rank (top 1, top 3, top 5) rather than the raw numeric score, as rank is stable relative to pool size.
What is the CSR qualification threshold?
CSR uses the expected qualified pitcher threshold: one inning pitched for every team game played during the season. This is the same standard applied to ERA qualifiers and aligns with the innings threshold Cy Young voters implicitly apply. For the 2026 season through June 3, the qualified pool contains 68 starting pitchers.
Why does CSR treat context as a feature rather than a flaw?
Because the Cy Young Award is a contextual award. Voters are not selecting the pitcher with the best talent estimate in a defense-neutral vacuum. They are selecting the pitcher who most dominated Major League hitters over a full season — in real ballparks, in front of real defenses, in real high-leverage situations. A metric that corrects all of that away is answering a question that no voter is asking. CSR keeps the context in RE24/IP, then corrects for the luck and defense contamination in that context through K-BB% and Barrel%.
