Who Is the 2026 MLB MVP? The Two-Way Ledger Through Early June.

If you have been defending an MVP pick off the home run leaderboard, that tool is why you keep arguing for the wrong name.

By Pete Dwyer  ·  Published June 2026
Quick Answer

As of early June 2026, Bobby Witt Jr. leads the AL MVP race at roughly 26 All-Around Runs, the widest margin in either league. James Wood leads the NL at about 19. The Two-Way Ledger combines offensive and defensive runs above average into a single number. It cannot be fooled by a loud bat bolted onto a leaking glove.

Why the Early MVP Conversation Keeps Getting It Wrong

Every June, the MVP conversation gets built out of a slash line and a home run total, and that describes a little under half of what a player actually does on a field. When the award later goes to a shortstop who saves a run and a half a week with his glove, the people who anchored to dingers act blindsided. The information was there the whole time. It just was not being counted in one place.

The belief underneath the standard take is that the Most Valuable Player is, give or take, the best hitter who plays every day. RBI get waved around, batting average gets a nod, and the eye test fills the gaps. For a long time that shortcut landed close to right by accident, because the best hitters were often complete players anyway.

The shortcut snaps the moment two players hit at a similar level and one of them is quietly handing runs back on defense while the other is saving them. Value is not a hitting contest. Value is everything a player adds with the bat and on the bases, plus everything he adds or costs with the glove, measured in runs. A 145 wRC+ catcher who frames and blocks and throws lives in a different tier than a 145 wRC+ first baseman who is a statue, and the June lists treat them as twins because both bats read loud.

So the real question is never who is hitting the most. It is who is producing the most total value, both halves included, right now.

What Is the Two-Way Ledger?

The Two-Way Ledger is a single number per player, expressed in runs above league average, that adds offensive value to defensive value. Offensive value is a hitter's weighted runs above average at the plate plus his baserunning. Defensive value is his fielding runs, with a positional adjustment baked in so a shortstop earns credit for a hard job and a designated hitter does not get a free pass. Add the two and you get All-Around Runs. The bigger the number, the more complete the player.

A few mile markers make the rankings read on their own. Roughly ten All-Around Runs through early June is a strong, clearly above-average regular. Fifteen is a star pace. Anything north of twenty by the first week of June is hardware territory if it holds.

The metric has the one quality the home run leaderboard lacks. It cannot be fooled by a loud bat bolted onto a leaking glove.

Two details in that list tend to stop people. And two of the top three are catchers, the position the MVP talk forgets exists until October.

The Case for Each AL Name

Bobby Witt Jr., KCR
AL No. 1 — ~26 All-Around Runs
Witt leads the AL because an elite glove at the sport's hardest position turns a good offensive season into a separation play. The bat is a 126 wRC+, the legs are nineteen steals, and the defense is the kind that quietly banks a run and a half a week. Stack those and the margin to second place is not close to anything else in this article.
Shea Langeliers, OAK
AL No. 2
The first catcher, slugging .534 with fourteen home runs and a 145 wRC+. His defensive value is modestly positive rather than loud, but the bat is strong enough that the glove only needs to avoid dragging, and it does.
Dillon Dingler, DET
AL No. 3
The .236 average keeps him off every conventional list, and that is the mistake. He is providing close to ten runs of defensive value behind the plate on top of thirteen home runs, and the ledger sees a player most fans are sleeping on because they are reading the wrong column.
Nick Kurtz, OAK
AL No. 4
This is the thesis of this whole piece wearing one name. Kurtz owns a 164 wRC+ and a .440 on-base percentage, the best bat in the league by a comfortable stretch, and his offensive value alone clears twenty runs. The defense at first costs several runs, and that subtraction is the only reason a hitter this good is sitting fourth. If he pulls the glove even to neutral, he climbs fast.
Mike Trout, LAA
AL No. 5
A vintage 150 wRC+ and a .411 on-base with fourteen homers. The center field defense no longer adds the way it once did, which trims a couple of runs and a couple of spots, but a healthy Trout at this level is still a top-five player in his league, and the story this year is that the health is holding.

One honesty note that matters for a metric people are meant to trust. Elly De La Cruz of the Reds would have ranked second on the raw numbers, and he is not in the active top five because he is on the injured list. A frozen stat line from a player who cannot take the field is not a live MVP case, so he comes off the active board until he returns.

The Case for Each NL Name

Andy Pages, LAD
NL No. 2

He hits .293 with a 141 wRC+ and fifty-one RBI, then plays six-plus runs of elite center field defense, and the combination vaults him past hitters with shinier slash lines. Pages is the strongest two-way profile in the National League and the name most people are leaving out of this conversation.
Xavier Edwards, MIA
NL No. 3
Hitting a tidy .309 with a 144 wRC+ and double-digit steals from second base, value built on contact, on-base, and legs rather than power. That is exactly the profile the home run board renders invisible.
Brandon Lowe, PIT
NL No. 4
The thump-and-glove version of the second base archetype. Fifteen homers and a .535 slug with positive defense at the keystone.
Corbin Carroll, ARI
NL No. 5
Slugging .545 with a 155 wRC+ and roughly neutral corner defense, a reminder that you can be one of the five best hitters in your league and still sit behind a center fielder with a better glove.

Who is the AL MVP favorite in 2026?

Who is the NL MVP favorite in 2026?

What is the Two-Way Ledger?

The Two-Way Ledger combines a player's offensive runs above average with defensive runs above average, including a positional adjustment. The result is All-Around Runs, a single number representing a player's complete value. Roughly ten All-Around Runs through early June is a strong regular. Fifteen is a star pace. North of twenty is hardware territory.

Why isn't the best hitter the MVP?

Because value counts both halves of the game. Nick Kurtz owns the best bat in the American League at a 164 wRC+, yet he ranks fourth because his first base defense gives runs back. A player with a slightly quieter bat and a strong glove at a hard position can be worth more in total.

Is Aaron Judge the 2026 AL MVP?

Aaron Judge is one of the two or three best hitters in the American League with a 150 wRC+, but he falls outside the top five on the Two-Way Ledger because his defensive value grades slightly negative, which costs him ground against complete players like Witt. The award measures total value, and right now the glove gap is the difference.

What stat combines hitting and defense for the MVP race?

All-Around Runs, the figure behind the Two-Way Ledger, combines offensive runs above average with defensive runs above average that include a positional adjustment. It expresses a position player's complete value in a single runs-based number.
