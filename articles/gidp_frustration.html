Quick Answer

What Does GIDP Mean in Baseball?

GIDP stands for Grounded Into Double Play. It is a batting statistic that counts how many times a hitter hit a ground ball that resulted in two outs on the same play. A batter is charged with a GIDP only when there is at least one runner on base and fewer than two outs. The most common form: a ground ball to the left side of the infield, thrown to second for the first out, then to first for the second. Lower is better. A GIDP is the most run-damaging outcome in baseball because it simultaneously wastes a plate appearance and eliminates a baserunner.

The Direct Answer

Why Is a Double Play So Frustrating?

A double play is frustrating because it instantly converts a base-runner opportunity into two outs while leaving the bases empty. It erases positive run expectancy in a single pitch, kills offensive momentum, and punishes hard contact on the ground — making it feel like doing everything right still produces nothing. In Bases Gained terms, each GIDP costs a hitter an estimated 2+ units of offensive value. Two outs. One pitch. The inning ends before it starts.

What the Bases Gained Lens Adds

The Gap Between Caminero and Naylor

Both sit near the top of the GIDP list. But Caminero's season-total RE24 is +13.0 — meaning that across every plate appearance, including the sixteen double plays, he has still added thirteen runs of value above a league-average hitter. Naylor's is −10.9. Same general category of problem, on opposite ends of the outcome.

That's a 24-run gap between two hitters separated by three GIDP. Caminero's estimated Bases Gained sits at 200 against Naylor's 133 — the 26 home runs and 48 walks are doing enough elsewhere that the double plays barely register. Naylor doesn't have that margin. His .358 slugging mark isn't producing enough on the other side of the ledger to absorb the cost the way Caminero's does.

Alvarez tells a third version of the same story. His RE24 (−7.3) sits between Caminero and Naylor, but he's compiled it in barely half the plate appearances of either one. Baseball Reference's own double-play-runs metric (Rdp) has him at −2 — identical to Naylor's mark, despite Naylor needing 359 plate appearances to get there and Alvarez needing 212. The independent, opportunity-adjusted stat backs up what the rate already suggested: this isn't just a counting-stat quirk. It's a real, recurring cost, and it's accumulating faster for Alvarez than for anyone else near the top of this list.

GIDP is not a standalone component of the Bases Gained formula — but it punishes a hitter across two separate channels. First, it eliminates a plate appearance that could have added total bases, walks, or RBI. Second, the RE24 component captures the run expectancy collapse when a double play converts a runner-on, one-out situation into bases empty, three outs. Both channels hit the ledger. The 2-unit penalty in the table above is conservative — and lines up with the direction, if not the exact magnitude, of Baseball Reference's own RE24 and Rdp figures.

The Bigger Picture

Why the Double Play Hurts More Than the Numbers Show

A strikeout costs one out. A GIDP costs two — and the psychological weight is heavier than the math. Hard contact that goes for nothing. A runner erased. A pitcher who walked away from a jam he should not have survived. The inning ending not with a soft tapper but with what looked, for a fraction of a second, like it might leave the park.

That is the frustration. The ball was hit well enough. The fielder was positioned correctly, the footwork was clean, and the runner was not fast enough to break it up. Baseball does not issue moral victories for hard contact on the ground.

The Bases Gained framework captures this precisely because it is a counting stat. A great at-bat three innings ago does not cancel what just happened. Each GIDP chips away at the ledger. Sixteen of them, as Caminero has accumulated, would be a hole most hitters couldn't climb out of — except his surrounding profile is strong enough to make it a footnote. Alvarez, with 12 in barely a third of a season, doesn't have that luxury yet.

"The Bases Gained framework is merciless to hitters who kill rallies. Each GIDP chips away at the ledger. Sixteen of them is a hole even a great season has to climb out of."

Common Questions

GIDP: Frequently Asked Questions

What is a good GIDP total in baseball?

Lower is always better. The league average for a full-season qualifying hitter runs roughly 10–14 GIDP. Heavy ground-ball hitters with pull tendencies and slow foot speed tend to lead the category. Elite fly-ball hitters and fast runners can finish a full season with fewer than 5. A hitter posting 10 GIDP before June is on pace well above league average.

What is GIDP probability in baseball?

GIDP probability measures the likelihood that a plate appearance will result in a grounded into double play. It is highest when a runner is on first base with fewer than two outs and the batter has a high ground ball rate and pull tendency. Statcast tracks GIDP opportunity rate alongside actual totals to show which hitters convert opportunities into double plays at above-average rates. A hitter can have a high GIDP total simply by creating more double play situations — it is not always a skill problem as much as a profile one.

Is GIDP the same as a double play?

Not exactly. A double play is any play that results in two outs. A GIDP specifically charges the batter when the double play originated from their ground ball. If a runner is caught stealing and the batter then strikes out, that is a double play but not a GIDP. The distinction matters because GIDP is a batting statistic charged to the hitter, while other double plays can result from baserunning or fielding decisions.

What does GIDP cost a hitter in run expectancy?

A GIDP in a runner-on-first, one-out situation collapses run expectancy dramatically. In a typical MLB run environment, a team with a runner on first and one out expects to score roughly 0.54 runs before the inning ends. After a GIDP — bases empty, three outs — that number drops to zero. The swing is over half a run in a single pitch. Multiply that across a full season and the cumulative damage shows up in a hitter's season-total RE24: Josh Naylor sits at −10.9 for 2026 largely because of it, while Junior Caminero, who leads MLB in GIDP outright, still carries a +13.0 RE24 because his power and walk rate are outproducing the cost.
