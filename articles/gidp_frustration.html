// Why is a double play so frustrating?

A double play is frustrating because it instantly converts a scoring opportunity into two outs while leaving the bases empty. It erases positive run expectancy in a single pitch, kills offensive momentum, and punishes hard contact on the ground — making it feel like doing everything right still produces nothing. In Bases Gained terms, each GIDP costs a hitter an estimated 2+ units of offensive value. Two outs. One pitch. The inning ends before it starts.

Baseball has a lot of ways to make an out. Some feel neutral. Some feel earned. A grounded into double play feels like a crime scene. The ball was hit hard. The runner was moving. And then — in the span of about three seconds — the inning is over and the dugout is quiet.

The numbers confirm what your eyes tell you. A GIDP does not just cost one out. It costs two outs, erases a base runner, and eliminates the run expectancy that built up over the first half of the inning. In the Bases Gained framework, that is a minimum 2-unit penalty against a hitter's seasonal contribution — before you even account for RE24 damage.

So who is leading the league in double plays through late May 2026? And what does it actually cost them?

What the table tells you

Ryan O'Hearn has managed 10 GIDP in just 182 plate appearances. That is one double play every 18.2 trips to the plate. Francisco Alvarez is even more alarming: 10 GIDP in 128 PA. One every 12.8 PA. At that rate, the catcher is eliminating a rally almost once a week before the season is half over.

Junior Caminero's 9 GIDP at 22 years old are worth watching. He is making contact at a rate that creates GIDP opportunities — which is a byproduct of his aggressive, pull-heavy approach. The question for Tampa Bay is whether the 13 home runs that come with that profile are worth the rally-killing that tags along.

Josh Jung's presence on this list is the most interesting item for Rangers fans. He is the only player in the top five posting a WAR above 1.0 — meaning his production elsewhere is overcoming the double play damage. For now.

GIDP Through the Bases Gained Lens

Standard box scores count GIDP. They do not tell you what it costs. That is where Bases Gained comes in.

The Bases Gained (BG) framework, developed by The Baseball Nerd, measures every direct action a hitter takes to create run-scoring opportunity: total bases, walks, hit by pitch, stolen bases (with a tiered caught stealing penalty), RBI minus home runs, and RE24. It is a counting stat, not a rate stat. It accumulates across a season. And it is merciless to hitters who kill rallies with ground balls.

GIDP is not a standalone component of the Bases Gained formula — but it punishes a hitter across two separate channels. First, it eliminates a plate appearance that could have added total bases, walks, or RBI. Second, the RE24 component captures the drop in run expectancy when a double play converts a runner-on, one-out situation into bases empty, three outs. Both hit the ledger. The estimated 2-unit GIDP penalty below represents the direct BG opportunity cost per occurrence.

What the Bases Gained lens adds

The gap between Caminero and Alvarez becomes obvious here. Both are near the top of the GIDP list. But Caminero has a BG of 106 against Alvarez's 37 — because Caminero has 97 total bases and a patient-enough approach at the plate to generate 29 walks. His GIDP count is a problem. His broader offensive profile is not.

Alvarez is the one that concerns the Mets most. Thirty-seven estimated Bases Gained in 128 plate appearances — a rate of 0.29 per PA — is well below league average for a catcher asked to hit in the middle of the order. The 10 GIDP are not just a quirky leaderboard entry. They are erasing production he simply has not built up enough elsewhere to absorb.

Jeremiah Jackson's 3 walks in 171 PA is its own story. He is walking roughly once every 57 PA while grounding into 9 double plays. That is not a GIDP problem. That is a plate approach problem that shows up in GIDP.

Why the Double Play Hurts More Than the Numbers Show

A strikeout costs one out. A GIDP costs two — and the psychological weight is heavier than the math. Hard contact that goes for nothing. A runner erased. A pitcher who walked away from a jam he should not have survived. The inning ending not with a soft tapper but with what looked, for a fraction of a second, like it might leave the park.

That is the frustration. The ball was hit well enough. The fielder just happened to be in the right place, the footwork was clean, and the runner was not fast enough to break it up. Baseball does not care about moral victories on contact.

The Bases Gained framework captures this precisely because it is a counting stat. A great at-bat three innings ago does not cancel what just happened. Each GIDP chips away at the ledger. Ten of them, as O'Hearn and Alvarez have accumulated, is a hole in the production line that the rest of the profile has to dig out of.
